Introduction to Universal Gravity


Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation

    Newton's law of universal gravitation was first promulgated in Sir Isaac Newton's book Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica in 1687. The law states that any two objects in the universe are attracted to each other, and the force of this attraction is proportional to the product of their masses and is inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. In other words, we can say gravity follows the inverse square law in relation to distance.

    Because all objects have a gravitational attraction to each other, it is correct to say an apple on the earth exerts as much gravitational force on the earth as the earth does on the apple. The reason why we see apples falling to the ground and not the other way around is because F=ma and because the mass of the earth is far greater than that of the apple.

    We can find the velocity of the Moon using the gravitational force formula and the centripetal force formula, which is F = ( Mmoon • v2 ) / r. Because these two forces are equal, we can set these two formula equal to each other to find v = sqrt(G * Mearth / r). In other words, the closer the Moon is to the Earth, the faster the Moon will orbit.

    As you may have noticed, the bodies orbited faster when they were closer to the barycenter.
